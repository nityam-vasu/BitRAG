# THE UNKNOWN - Chapter One

## The Arrival

The train whistle screamed like something wounded as it pulled into Ravenshollow Station, a mournful sound that seemed to echo off the surrounding hills and return multiplied. Dr. Elena Vance pressed her palm against the grimy window, watching the small coastal town emerge from the October fog like a ghost taking shape from mist.

Ravenshollow. She hadn't spoken the name in twenty years, hadn't allowed herself to think it except in her darkest moments, in the small hours when memories of her mother refused to stay buried. And now here she was, returning for the first time since she was twelve years old, summoned by a letter from a dead man.

The letter had arrived three days ago, bearing the Blackwood family seal. Inside, a single key and a message: "Your uncle Marcus has died. He left something for you. Come before the fog of All Hallows, or it will be too late. Come alone."

Elena had read those words a hundred times. The handwriting was not Marcus's—she'd found records confirming he'd died in August—but the key was real enough. And so was the return address: Blackwood Manor.

The train ground to a halt with a shudder that rattled Elena's teeth. Through the window, she could see the platform: empty except for a single figure standing beneath the amber glow of a gas lamp. The figure was draped in black, face obscured by the fog that rolled in from somewhere beyond the station building.

"Dr. Vance?" The voice was male, aged, careful. "I've been sent to collect you."

Elena gathered her leather satchel—she'd packed light, as instructed—and stepped onto the platform. The cold hit her immediately, sharp with the smell of salt and something else, something older. The sea, she told herself. Just the sea.

The man before her was older than she'd expected from his voice, perhaps sixty, with a face like weathered leather and eyes that seemed to take her measure in a single glance. He wore a long black coat, and his hands, when he reached for her bag, were trembling.

"I'm Thomas Blackwood's assistant," he said. "You can call me Jeremiah."

"Thomas Blackwood?" Elena frowned. "I was expecting—"

"The master of the house will receive you in due time," Jeremiah interrupted, his tone making it clear the conversation was over. "This way, Dr. Vance. The car is waiting."

There was no car. There was only a horse-drawn carriage, black as a hearse, waiting at the station's edge. Elena hesitated, but the fog was thickening around her, pressing in from all sides, and she could swear she heard something in it—a whisper, perhaps, or just the wind.

She climbed into the carriage.

---

## The Road to Blackwood Manor

The journey took nearly an hour, though the distance couldn't have been more than a few miles. The road wound along the cliff edge, and Elena could hear the waves crashing against the rocks below, though she couldn't see them through the fog. Twice, Jeremiah turned to look at her, his face pale in the carriage lantern's glow, but he said nothing.

"Your uncle spoke of you often," he said finally, as the carriage crested a hill and the first glimpse of Blackwood Manor appeared through the mist.

Elena's heart clenched. "I never knew my uncle Marcus. He was my mother's brother, wasn't he?"

Jeremiah's hands tightened on the reins. "He was. A brilliant man. Too brilliant, perhaps. The things he found in the manor's depths..." He trailed off, shaking his head. "You'll understand soon enough, Dr. Vance. That's why you were summoned."

"What exactly did he leave me?"

"A box. Locked. He said only you could open it—that the key would know its owner." Jeremiah glanced back at her, and for a moment, she saw something like pity in his eyes. "He also left a warning. The third night after your arrival, go to the east wing. The door will be open. But be careful what you find there, Dr. Vance. The manor has a way of showing people what they're afraid to see."

Before Elena could respond, the carriage stopped. They had arrived.

Blackwood Manor rose before her like a fever dream, all sharp angles and dark windows, its Victorian spires stabbing into the fog-choked sky. The building was massive—far larger than she'd expected—and far older. Parts of it looked medieval, grafted onto a Victorian framework like bones showing through diseased skin. The front door was a massive oak structure, banded with iron, and as Elena watched, it began to open.

Standing in the doorway was a man in his late fifties, his silver hair combed back from a face that might have been handsome if not for the coldness in his eyes. He wore a black suit, and his posture was rigid, almost military.

"Dr. Vance." His voice was clipped, formal. "I am Thomas Blackwood. Welcome to your family home."

---

## The Master of Blackwood

Thomas Blackwood's study was everything Elena had expected and nothing like it. Mahogany shelves lined the walls, filled with books whose spines bore titles in languages she didn't recognize—Latin, Greek, something older still. A fire burned in the massive hearth, but it provided little warmth; the room felt cold, somehow, as if the chill came from within.

"Sit down, Dr. Vance." Thomas gestured to a leather chair. "We have much to discuss."

Elena remained standing. "I'd prefer to stand, thank you. I've traveled a long way, and I'd like to understand why I'm here."

Thomas's lips thinned. "Very well. My brother Marcus is dead. He died three months ago, in this house, under... unusual circumstances. Before his death, he asked that you be contacted. He left you something—a box, containing his research. He believed you were the only one who could understand it."

"Why me? I barely knew the man."

"That's between you and Marcus." Thomas moved to his desk, opened a drawer, and produced a small wooden box. It was plain, unadorned, but as Elena took it, she felt something—a pulse, almost, like a heartbeat, emanating from within.

"The key," Thomas said. "It will only open for you."

Elena reached into her pocket and withdrew the key the letter had promised. As she held it against the box's lock, she felt that pulse again, stronger now, and then—click. The lid swung open.

Inside lay a single journal, leather-bound, and a photograph. Elena picked up the photograph first, and her breath caught.

It was her mother. Younger than Elena had ever known her, standing before Blackwood Manor, pregnant. And behind her, half-hidden by the fog, stood seven figures. Their faces were blurred, but Elena could make out their shapes—men, women, children.

"The Seven," Thomas said quietly. "They vanished in 1894. Your mother was there when it happened."

Elena looked up, her heart pounding. "What happened to them? What happened to my mother?"

Thomas's face was unreadable. "That's what Marcus was trying to discover. And now, Dr. Vance, I believe it's your turn to find out."

At that moment, the lights flickered. And in the darkness, Elena could have sworn she heard something—a whisper, right at the edge of hearing, forming words she almost understood.

*Welcome home, Elena. Welcome home.*

---

## The Night Visitor

Elena barely slept that first night. The room Thomas had given her was large and elegantly furnished, but it felt wrong somehow, as if the walls themselves were watching her. She kept the journal and photograph beside her on the bed, reading them over and over, searching for clues.

Her mother had come to Blackwood Manor in September 1894, pregnant with Elena. She'd been here for the Blackwood Festival, an annual gathering of the town's elite. But something had gone wrong. Seven people had vanished over the course of a single week, and when the fog finally cleared, Catherine Vance was gone too—leaving behind only her infant daughter.

Elena had always been told her mother had abandoned her, had run off with another man. Now she knew the truth: her mother hadn't left. She'd been taken. And somehow, Elena herself was connected to what had happened here.

Sometime after midnight, a soft knock came at her door.

Elena opened it to find a young woman standing in the corridor. She was beautiful, with dark hair and darker eyes, and she was shaking.

"You need to leave," the woman whispered. "Before the third night. Before the fog comes in."

"Who are you?"

"Adelaide. Adelaide Blackwood. And I know what you're looking for. But some answers are too dangerous to find." She pressed something into Elena's hand—a folded piece of paper. "Read this. Then burn it. And please, Dr. Vance—leave while you still can."

Before Elena could respond, Adelaide turned and vanished into the fog-filled corridor.

Elena unfolded the paper. Inside, in a shaky hand, were written two words:

*THE WATCHER*
